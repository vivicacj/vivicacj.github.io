🧬 AKM #1.1: The Role of Accounting & Stakeholders

Syllabus: 1.1, 1.2 Priority: ★ Must-Know

🧠 1. Academic Core Concept

Accounting is the "language of business." It is an information system that identifies, records, and communicates the economic events of a business to interested users, known as stakeholders. The primary role is to provide information for decision-making.

✍️ 2. Exam Answer Toolkit

To define the role of accounting:

"The role of accounting is to provide financial information that is useful for decision-making by various stakeholders, such as owners and managers."

To identify stakeholders:

Stakeholders are groups or individuals with an interest in the performance of a business. They can be internal (e.g., managers, employees) or external (e.g., investors, banks, suppliers, government).

Stakeholder Decision-Making Table:

Stakeholder Example Decision Information Needed
Owner Should I continue to invest in the business? Is the business profitable? Profit for the year, Owner's Equity
Manager How can we improve performance? Which products are most profitable? Sales figures, Cost of sales, Expenses
Bank/Lender Should we approve a loan for the business? Can it repay its debts? Existing liabilities, Profitability, Liquidity ratios
Supplier Should we sell goods on credit to this business? Will they pay on time? Current liabilities, Cash/Bank balance

⚠️ 3. Common Errors & Pitfalls

⚠️
Mistake: Simply listing stakeholders without explaining their specific interest.
Click to see correction →
✓ Correction: You must link the stakeholder to the type of decision they need to make. For example, "A supplier is interested in the business's liquidity to assess if the business can pay its debts on time."

❓ 4. Typical G3 Practice Questions & Answers

Q: State two stakeholders of a business and explain why each is interested in its financial information. ▼

A:

  1. Stakeholder: The owner. Reason: The owner is interested in the profitability of the business to decide whether to continue investing in it or to withdraw their capital. They would look at the Profit for the Year.
  2. Stakeholder: A bank. Reason: A bank is interested in the business's ability to repay loans. It would look at the business's existing liabilities and its cash position to assess the risk of lending money.

🏢 AKM #1.2: Business Entities & Types

Syllabus: 2.1, 2.2 Priority: ★ Must-Know

🧠 1. Academic Core Concept

Forms of Business Ownership:

  • Sole Proprietorship: A business owned by one person. The owner has unlimited liability, and the business is not a separate legal entity.
  • Partnership: A business owned by two to twenty partners. Partners typically have unlimited liability.
  • Private Limited Company (Pte Ltd): A business owned by shareholders (1 to 50). It is a separate legal entity, and shareholders have limited liability.

Types of Business Activities:

  • Trading Business: Buys and sells goods (e.g., a retail shop). Its main income is Sales Revenue, and it has Cost of Sales.
  • Service Business: Provides services (e.g., a hair salon, a law firm). Its main income is Service Fee Revenue and it has no Cost of Sales.

✍️ 2. Exam Answer Toolkit

Sole Proprietorship vs. Private Limited Company:

Feature Sole Proprietorship Private Limited Company
Liability Unlimited liability (Owner's personal assets are at risk) Limited liability (Shareholders' personal assets are protected)
Legal Status Not a separate legal entity A separate legal entity
Owner's Claim Capital / Owner's Equity Share Capital & Retained Earnings
Profit Distribution Profit is added to Capital Profit is added to Retained Earnings; may be distributed as Dividends
Withdrawal Drawings Dividends (formal)

⚠️ 3. Common Errors & Pitfalls

⚠️
Mistake: Confusing "unlimited liability" with "separate legal entity".
Click to see correction →
✓ Correction:
  • Liability is about risk: Are the owner's personal assets (car, house) at risk if the business fails? (Unlimited = Yes; Limited = No).
  • Legal Entity is about identity: Can the business sue or be sued in its own name? (Separate Legal Entity = Yes).

❓ 4. Typical G3 Practice Questions & Answers

Q: John wants to start a small bakery. He is worried about losing his personal savings if the business fails. Which form of business ownership would you recommend and why? ▼

A: I would recommend a Private Limited Company. The key feature is limited liability, which means that if the business fails, John is only at risk of losing the amount he invested in the company's shares. His personal savings would be protected.

⚖️ AKM #1.3: Professional Ethics (Integrity & Objectivity)

Syllabus: 1.1 Priority: ☆ Secondary

🧠 1. Academic Core Concept

Integrity: The quality of being straightforward and honest in all professional and business relationships. It implies fair dealing and truthfulness.

Objectivity: The quality of not allowing bias, conflict of interest, or the undue influence of others to override professional judgments. Decisions must be based on facts.

✍️ 2. Exam Answer Toolkit

"An accountant must show integrity by ensuring that all financial reports are truthful and do not mislead users."

"An accountant must maintain objectivity by not letting their personal relationship with a manager influence their decision to, for example, overstate the company's assets."

⚠️ 3. Common Errors & Pitfalls

⚠️
Mistake: Giving a vague definition like "being a good person."
Click to see correction →
✓ Correction: You must use the keywords. Integrity = Honesty. Objectivity = No Bias.

❓ 4. Typical G3 Practice Questions & Answers

Q (Meridian Paper 1): State and explain one accounting ethic accountants must uphold. ▼

A: One ethic is Integrity. This means an accountant must be straightforward and honest in all professional relationships. For example, they should not knowingly prepare a financial statement with false figures to deceive a bank.

🧱 AKM #3.1: Elements of Financial Statements

Syllabus: 2.3 Priority: ★ Must-Know

🧠 1. Academic Core Concept

The five elements of accounting are the building blocks used to record all business transactions:

  1. Assets (A): Economic resources controlled by the business as a result of past events, from which future economic benefits are expected to flow to the business. (What the business owns).
  2. Liabilities (L): Present obligations of the business arising from past events, the settlement of which is expected to result in an outflow of economic benefits. (What the business owes).
  3. Equity (E): The residual interest in the assets of the business after deducting all its liabilities. E = A - L. (The owner's claim on the assets).
  4. Income (I): Increases in economic benefits during the accounting period in the form of inflows or enhancements of assets, or decreases of liabilities, that result in increases in equity, other than those relating to contributions from equity participants. (What the business earns).
  5. Expenses (X): Decreases in economic benefits during the accounting period in the form of outflows or depletions of assets, or incurrences of liabilities, that result in decreases in equity, other than those relating to distributions to equity participants. (Costs incurred to earn income).

✍️ 2. Exam Answer Toolkit

Common Examples for G3:

Element Sub-category Examples
Assets Non-current Assets (> 1 year) Office Equipment, Motor Vehicles, Fixtures & Fittings, Premises
Current Assets (< 1 year) Inventory, Trade Receivables, Cash at Bank, Cash in Hand
Liabilities Non-current Liabilities (> 1 year) Long-term Bank Loan
Current Liabilities (< 1 year) Trade Payables, Bank Overdraft
Equity Sole Proprietorship Capital
Income Revenue from main activity Sales revenue, Service fee revenue
Other Income Rent income, Commission income, Discount received
Expenses Cost of running the business Cost of sales, Rent expense, Salaries, Utilities, Discount allowed

⚠️ 3. Common Errors & Pitfalls

⚠️
Mistake: Confusing Trade Receivables and Trade Payables.
Click to see correction →
✓ Correction:
  • Receivables: We are going to Receive cash from customers who owe us. It's an Asset.
  • Payables: We have to Pay cash to our suppliers whom we owe. It's a Liability.
⚠️
Mistake: Confusing Discount Allowed with Discount Received.
Click to see correction →
✓ Correction:
  • Allowed: We allowed a customer to pay less. This reduces our income. It's an Expense.
  • Received: Our supplier allowed us to pay less. This reduces our expense. It's Other Income.

❓ 4. Typical G3 Practice Questions & Answers

Q: Classify the following items into Asset, Liability, Equity, Income, or Expense: (a) Motor vehicle, (b) A loan from the bank, (c) Goods sold on credit. ▼

A:

  • (a) Motor vehicle: Asset (specifically, a Non-current Asset).
  • (b) A loan from the bank: Liability.
  • (c) Goods sold on credit: This transaction involves two elements. The sale itself is Income (Sales revenue), and the amount owed by the customer (Trade receivable) is an Asset.

⚖️ AKM #3.2: The Accounting Equation in Practice

Syllabus: 2.4 Priority: ★ Must-Know

🧠 1. Academic Core Concept

Every transaction creates a dual effect that keeps the equation Assets = Liabilities + Equity in balance. The four possible types of effects are:

  1. One Asset ↑, another Asset ↓ (e.g., Buy equipment with cash)
  2. Asset ↑, Liability or Equity ↑ (e.g., Buy goods on credit)
  3. Asset ↓, Liability or Equity ↓ (e.g., Pay a supplier)
  4. One L/E item ↑, another L/E item ↓ (e.g., Convert a loan to owner's capital)

✍️ 2. Exam Answer Toolkit

SOP: Analyzing a Transaction's Effect

Question Format: "State the effect of the following transaction on the accounting equation."
1
Name the first account affected and its element type (A, L, or E). State whether it increases or decreases and by how much.
2
Name the second account affected and its element type. State whether it increases or decreases and by how much.
3
Conclude by stating that "The accounting equation remains balanced."

Example Application:

Transaction: The business bought a motor vehicle for $20,000, paying by cheque.

Answer:

  1. The asset Motor Vehicles will increase by $20,000.
  2. The asset Cash at Bank will decrease by $20,000.
  3. The accounting equation remains balanced.

Transaction: The business took a loan of $10,000 from the bank.

Answer:

  1. The asset Cash at Bank will increase by $10,000.
  2. The liability Bank Loan will increase by $10,000.
  3. The accounting equation remains balanced.

⚠️ 3. Common Errors & Pitfalls

⚠️
Mistake: Forgetting that Expenses and Revenues are part of Equity.
Click to see correction →
✓ Correction: When a transaction involves an expense or revenue, the effect on Equity must be stated.

Transaction: Received $500 cash for services provided.

Correct Answer: "The asset Cash in hand will increase by $500. Equity will increase by $500 because income increases profit."

❓ 4. Typical G3 Practice Questions & Answers

Q: A business has Assets of $50,000 and Capital of $30,000. Calculate liabilities. The business then buys inventory worth $5,000 on credit. Calculate the new values for A, L, and E. ▼

A:

  1. Initial Liabilities: Using A = L + E, $50,000 = L + $30,000. Therefore, Liabilities = $20,000.
  2. Transaction Analysis:
    • Buying inventory (Asset) increases Assets.
    • Buying on credit (Trade Payable) increases Liabilities.
  3. New Values:
    • New Assets: $50,000 + $5,000 = $55,000.
    • New Liabilities: $20,000 + $5,000 = $25,000.
    • New Equity: Remains unchanged at $30,000.
  4. Check: $55,000 (A) = $25,000 (L) + $30,000 (E). It balances.

🔄 AKM #4.1: The Double-Entry System & DEAD CLIC

Syllabus: 2.4, 3.3 Priority: ★ Must-Know

🧠 1. Academic Core Concept

The Double-Entry Rule: Every transaction affects at least two accounts. To keep the accounting equation in balance, the total amount debited must equal the total amount credited for every transaction.

T-Account: The visual representation of a ledger account, with a debit (Dr) side on the left and a credit (Cr) side on the right.

The Modern Golden Rule (DEAD CLIC): A memory aid to determine whether to debit or credit an account.

DEAD CLIC Table:

Acronym Stands For To INCREASE To DECREASE
D.E.A.D. Drawings, Expenses, Assets DEBIT (Dr) Credit (Cr)
C.L.I.C. Capital, Liabilities, Income CREDIT (Cr) Debit (Dr)

✍️ 2. Exam Answer Toolkit

SOP: The 4-Step Process for Recording Any Transaction

1 Identify the Two Accounts: Read the transaction and name the two accounts involved. (e.g., "Cash at Bank", "Sales Revenue").
2 Classify Each Account: Use your knowledge from AKM #3.1. Is it an Asset, Liability, Equity, Income, or Expense?
3 Determine the Effect & Apply DEAD CLIC:
  • Is the first account increasing or decreasing? Based on its classification, should you Debit or Credit it?
  • Is the second account increasing or decreasing? Based on its classification, should you Debit or Credit it?
4 Construct the Journal Entry: Write the Debit entry on the first line and the Credit entry indented on the second line.

Example Application:

Transaction: Paid rent of $1,200 by cheque.

  1. Identify: "Rent Expense" and "Cash at Bank".
  2. Classify: "Rent Expense" is an Expense. "Cash at Bank" is an Asset.
  3. Apply DEAD CLIC:
    • Rent Expense is increasing. To increase an Expense, you Debit. → Dr Rent Expense $1,200.
    • Cash at Bank is decreasing. To decrease an Asset, you Credit. → Cr Cash at Bank $1,200.
Dr  Rent Expense        1,200
    Cr  Cash at Bank          1,200

⚠️ 3. Common Errors & Pitfalls

Mistake: Reversing the debit and credit entries. This is the most common error for beginners.
✓ Correction: Slow down and rigorously apply the 4-step process. Do not guess. Write down "A, L, E, I, or X" next to each account name as you analyze it. Say the DEAD CLIC rule out loud.
Mistake: Confusing the business's books with your personal bank account. Thinking "Credit" always means "plus money."
✓ Correction: In accounting, "Credit" does not mean "good" or "plus." It is simply the name for the right-hand side of a T-account. Its effect (increase or decrease) depends entirely on the account type (DEAD or CLIC).
Mistake: When a transaction involves cash, students often only think about the cash account and forget the other side of the transaction.
✓ Correction: Force yourself to always identify two accounts. The question is never just "What happens to cash?" It's "What did we get, or what did we give up, in exchange for the cash?"

❓ 4. Typical G3 Practice Questions & Answers

Q: For each transaction, state the account to be debited and the account to be credited: (a) Bought equipment for $3,000 on credit. (b) Owner took $200 cash for personal use. ▼

A:

(a) Analysis:

  • Accounts: "Office Equipment" (Asset) and "Trade Payables" (Liability).
  • Effect: Office Equipment is increasing. Trade Payables is increasing.
  • DEAD CLIC: To increase an Asset, you Debit. To increase a Liability, you Credit.
  • Answer: Debit Office Equipment, Credit Trade Payables.

(b) Analysis:

  • Accounts: "Drawings" (Equity) and "Cash in hand" (Asset).
  • Effect: Drawings is increasing. Cash in hand is decreasing.
  • DEAD CLIC: To increase Drawings, you Debit. To decrease an Asset, you Credit.
  • Answer: Debit Drawings, Credit Cash in hand.

🧾 AKM #5: Source Documents & Journals

Syllabus: 1.3, 2.4, 3.2 Priority: ★ Must-Know

🧠 1. Academic Core Concept

The Accounting Information System Cycle: A structured process to ensure transactions are recorded accurately. For G3, the key sequence is:

  1. Transaction Occurs → Evidenced by a Source Document.
  2. Analyze Source Document → Record in the Journal (Book of Prime Entry).
  3. Post Journal Entries → Transfer to individual Ledger Accounts (T-Accounts).
  4. Balance Ledgers → Prepare a Trial Balance.

The General Journal: The primary book for recording all transactions before posting to the ledger. It shows the date, the accounts to be debited and credited, the amounts, and a brief explanation (narration).

✍️ 2. Exam Answer Toolkit

General Journal Template:

Date Particulars / Details Debit ($) Credit ($)
[Date] [Account to be Debited]
[Account to be Credited]
(Narration: A brief explanation) 		[Amount] [Amount]

SOP: Writing a Narration

1 Be Brief and Clear: State what happened.
2 Mention the Source Document: If known, reference the invoice or receipt number.
✓ Example: (Being goods purchased on credit from Supplier XYZ, as per invoice 456)

⚠️ 3. Common Errors & Pitfalls

⚠️
Mistake: Forgetting the narration in a journal entry.
Click to see correction →
✓ Correction: Unless the question explicitly states "Narration is not required," you should always include a brief one. It explains the "story" behind the numbers.
⚠️
Mistake: Mixing up the format, putting the credit account name before the debit account name.
Click to see correction →
✓ Correction: The convention is strict: Debit entry first, then the indented credit entry below it.

❓ 4. Typical G3 Practice Questions & Answers

Q (Fuhua Paper 1, Q4c): Prepare journal entries without narrations to record the expenditures (Purchase of delivery truck $70,000, Import tax $6,500, Freight charges $1,400, Annual road tax $700, Petrol $100). ▼

A (Applying Capital vs. Revenue expenditure rules):

Journal

-----|-------------------------|-----------|-----------
Jan 1| Motor Vehicles          |    77,900 |
     | Road Tax                |       700 |
     | Petrol                  |       100 |
     |   Cash at Bank          |           |     78,700
     | (Working: Capital cost = 70000+6500+1400)

📊 AKM #6: Trial Balance Construction

Syllabus: 2.5, 3.2 Priority: ★ Must-Know

🧠 1. Academic Core Concept

The Trial Balance is a list of all ledger account balances at a specific date. It is prepared to verify the arithmetical accuracy of the double-entry system. A correctly prepared trial balance will have equal total debits and total credits.

✍️ 2. Exam Answer Toolkit

SOP: Preparing a Trial Balance

1 Create the Format: Title (Trial Balance as at [Date]) and two columns: Debit ($) and Credit ($).
2 Go Through the List of Accounts: For each account, determine its normal balance.
3 Apply DEAD CLIC to Balances:
  • Does the account fall under Drawings, Expenses, or Assets? Place its balance in the DEBIT column.
  • Does the account fall under Capital, Liabilities, or Income? Place its balance in the CREDIT column.
4 Handle Special Cases (Contra Accounts):
  • Sales Returns is an expense-like account → Debit.
  • Purchase Returns is an income-like account → Credit.
  • Accumulated Depreciation is a contra-asset → Credit.
  • Allowance for Impairment is a contra-asset → Credit.
5 Total Both Columns: Sum up the Debit column and the Credit column. The two totals must be equal.

⚠️ 3. Common Errors & Pitfalls

⚠️
Mistake: Placing an account balance in the wrong column. (e.g., putting Sales in Debit or Purchases in Credit).
Click to see correction →
✓ Correction: Drill the DEAD CLIC rule relentlessly. Purchases is an Expense (Debit). Sales is an Income (Credit).
⚠️
Mistake: Incorrectly placing contra accounts like Sales Returns or Accumulated Depreciation.
Click to see correction →
✓ Correction: Memorize the special cases. Sales Returns behaves like an expense (reduces income), so it has a Debit balance. Accumulated Depreciation reduces an asset, so it has a Credit balance (the opposite of an asset).

❓ 4. Typical G3 Practice Questions & Answers

Q: From the following list of balances, prepare a trial balance for ABC Trading as at 31 Dec 2023: Capital $20,000; Cash at Bank $5,000; Equipment $15,000; Sales $30,000; Purchases $25,000; Trade Payables $5,000. ▼

A:

Account Debit ($) Credit ($)

Cash at Bank5,000
Equipment15,000
